Rock & Roar

Translating abstract site analysis into concrete spatial decisions — circulation, program, and material — under real-world ecological constraints. Developed in the Landscape Architecture studio during my exchange semester at UC Berkeley.

03.
Framing the Design Idea
Seasonal seal presence creates a recurring human–wildlife conflict.

The design mediates it through spatial organization — discouraging intrusion while preserving openness and coexistence.

Caesar Chavez Park  ·  Design site (NE tip)
A   The Design Problem

A recurring human–wildlife conflict

Harbor seals use the northeastern shoreline as a haul-out zone during pupping season. The standard response — temporary fencing — is disruptive and ecologically incomplete. The goal: a permanent solution that holds both uses simultaneously.

Zone A — Human
Observation & Gathering
Seating terraces and framed viewpoints — close enough to see, far enough not to disturb
Zone B — Wildlife
Seal Haul-Out
Rocky shoreline kept undisturbed; structures are removable for pupping season
B   Sectional Strategy

The 17-foot gradient

The site drops 17 feet from inland path to waterline. Upper elevation = human zone; lower = seal territory. The stepped stone structure becomes a threshold: an invitation to look, not to descend.

  • Permanent stone terracing — haul-out surface below, viewing steps above
  • Portable wooden platform — present off-season, removed during pupping
  • Cladophora glomerata (water-cleaning algae) integrated into stone surface

C   Design Moves

Four moves,
one logic

Each decision serves the human experience and the ecological objective simultaneously.

1
Mediated Visual Access
Sightlines to the haul-out zone are framed, not fenced — intimate without intrusion.
2
Circulation as Nudge
Curved path system steers visitors toward observation nodes, away from sensitive edges.
3
Elevation as Permission
Stepped massing encodes hierarchy: upper = human, lower = wildlife.
4
Temporal Switching
The removable platform converts the same footprint between two programs across seasons.
Design Principle

Not choosing between access and protection — designing a space that holds both, using spatial gradient, material transition, and temporal scheduling as instruments of mediation.

Project Outcome

Insight into
spatial form

Berkeley Rock & Roar is the "Design" phase of the problem-solving cycle — translating system insight into spatial decisions that hold under real-world constraints. The physical arrangement of paths, materials, and elevations produces behavior without instruction.

Spatial gradient encodes permission
Elevation creates implicit boundaries without barriers
Material mediates behavior
Path → stone terrace → rocky shore signals decreasing human presence
Temporal design extends function
Same footprint serves two programs across seasons
Ecology embedded in material
Water-cleaning algae in stone — ecological function made structural
