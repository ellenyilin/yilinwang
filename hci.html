Health Ergonomics

Comfort Region Mapping for Repeated VR Interaction

In this project, I investigated how ergonomic load can be quantified in VR interactions, then translated experimental findings into actionable design guidelines. When a system-level issue is identified, I explore possible interventions through controlled experiments — this project is the "Experiment" stage of the Insight → Validate cycle.

VR interaction hasn't been widely adopted partly because repeated use is physically taxing. By running a structured experiment with 12 participants, I produced design-ready guidelines for VR UI placement that reduce fatigue and improve usability for long-duration tasks.

A   Research Questions

What we set out to measure

The experiment targeted three specific unknowns about ergonomic load in VR target-selection tasks — moving from broad discomfort to actionable spatial and distance recommendations.

RQ 1
Which interaction distance reduces physical load the most?
RQ 2
Which regions reduce mental search effort?
RQ 3
Do handedness and height shift preference patterns?

Experiment design

VariableLevels
Distance (IV) 0.45m · 0.60m · 0.75m
Region (IV) −60° to +60° in 30° increments (5 × 5 grid)
Workload (DV) NASA-TLX (6-dimensional)
Exertion (DV) Borg CR10 rating per trial
Qualitative (DV) Post-trial comfort notes + interview

Adapted from Wu et al., 2024, "Toward Ergonomic VR Target Selection." Within-subjects design; all participants completed all conditions. Manual assistance offered for hard-to-reach positions.

Consistent Pattern

Upper regions (positive vertical angles) score high across all dimensions. Lower-central regions consistently score lowest — especially at 0,−30 and ±30,−30.

ANOVA Result

Distance had a significant effect on perceived workload and exertion (p < .001 across most dimensions). Closer distances (0.45m) reduced physical load.

C   Design Insights

Three actionable guidelines

Combining quantitative heatmap data with qualitative interview findings, the experiment produced three concrete placement guidelines for VR UI designers.

1
Keep targets within the field of view
"It's really important that the target stays within my field of view. Turning my head was the most uncomfortable part."
Targets outside the FOV force head rotation, disproportionately increasing neck and shoulder load. Stay within ±30° horizontally.
2
Prefer lower and slightly right regions
"Targets in the lower area felt much more comfortable, especially on the right side."
Lower-central regions show consistently lower exertion across all five NASA-TLX dimensions. Right-side bias reflects dominant-hand preference in right-handed users.
3
Vary interaction regions over time
For long-duration or repetitive VR tasks, avoid concentrating interactions in a single fixed area. Rotating target placement across the comfort zone distributes muscular load and prevents fatigue accumulation.
Design Output

The outcome is a set of design-ready placement guidelines for VR UI that can reduce fatigue, improve comfort, and support long-duration interaction — grounded in experimental evidence rather than intuition.

Project Outcome

Quantifying ergonomics

This project demonstrates the "Experiment" phase of the problem-solving cycle — using controlled conditions to produce evidence that generalizes beyond individual observations.

The findings directly inform where VR designers should place UI elements to minimize physical and cognitive load. More broadly, the project shows how human factors research can be structured to produce actionable product guidelines rather than academic observations.
