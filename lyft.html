NYC Rideshare Traffic Analysis

My role in this project focused on recognizing where the transport system was underperforming and making that visible through data. By applying statistical analysis to NYC TLC trip records, I identified recurring patterns in temporal demand, spatial supply gaps, and pricing structure — ultimately reframing the problem from "low volume" to "misallocated supply during predictable peak windows."

A   Temporal Patterns

Demand has time patterns

Lyft's hourly data reveals two distinct demand profiles. Weekdays show clear commuter peaks at 7–9 AM and 5–7 PM; weekends are flatter, with activity concentrated in afternoons and evenings. This predictability is strategically significant — demand is foreseeable, but Lyft's supply response consistently lags.

  • When total demand surges, Lyft consistently loses share to Uber, indicating insufficient driver supply at peak hours
  • Between 15:00–18:00, total trip volume peaks while Lyft's market share falls to ~23% — its lowest point
  • Weekend demand is flatter, with elevated activity during afternoon and evening leisure hours
Finding

Demand is predictable. The problem is not uncertainty — it's a systematic failure to pre-position supply ahead of known peak windows.

The same pattern, across every borough

↳   Special Case: Staten Island

The outlier

Staten Island shows the opposite pattern: when total demand increases, Lyft actually gains share rather than losing it.

  • Smallest trip volume among all boroughs
  • Demand-share relationship is inverted — Lyft gains when demand rises
  • Reveals a structural supply shortage: any available driver is immediately utilized
  • Absence of competition makes Lyft the default when supply is present
Implication

Absolute supply scarcity can paradoxically improve share — validating that supply constraints, not demand, are the binding constraint system-wide.

C   Pricing & Trip Distance Structure

Lyft's core: low-price, short trips

Pricing and distance data reveal what types of trips Lyft predominantly captures — and why that makes peak-hour share loss especially costly.

  • Lyft charges a lower per-mile price for short trips (0–5 miles), creating a price-based selection effect
  • Lyft receives disproportionately high short trips (0–2 mi); KDE distribution confirms a sharp peak not seen in Uber
  • Pricing and demand reinforce each other — lower short-trip pricing attracts more short-trip riders, concentrating Lyft in low-revenue segments
Core Insight

Lyft's business is concentrated in low-price, short-distance trips — making peak-hour supply failure financially more damaging than it appears from share numbers alone.

D   Spatial Finding — Airport Priority Paradox

Finding: Priority contrast of 2 airports

Spatial analysis of driver zone priority scores revealed a striking anomaly — Lyft's algorithm and driver behavior operate on completely different utility functions.

LaGuardia: #1 → #62    JFK: #3 → #1

JFK Airport

Major international hub. Long trips, high revenue — but drivers face 60–150 min queues and long dead miles back. Most drivers voluntarily avoid it.

LaGuardia Airport

Closer to Manhattan. Short trips (6–12 mi), fast queue (30–90 min), virtually guaranteed next ride. Drivers self-organize and flood the zone.

The Paradox

The algorithm is financially correct, but driver behavior is utility-correct. JFK offers higher revenue per trip, but its friction (queue time, dead miles) drives drivers away — creating a supply shortage at a high-demand location without any platform signal to correct it.

Project Outcome

Reframing the problem space

The analysis revealed a clear contrast between temporal and spatial performance. Lyft's challenge was not a lack of demand or supply — but a misalignment between when and where supply was available versus when demand peaked.

Rather than prescribing a single solution, this reframing defined the problem space and set the stage for targeted intervention design.

Supply is misallocated, not insufficient
Drivers are present in the system but positioned in wrong zones or inactive during known peak windows
Demand is predictable
Weekday commuter peaks at 7–9 AM and 5–7 PM are consistent — this is a solvable scheduling problem
Algorithmic vs behavioral incentive gap
The JFK/LGA case shows platform incentives and driver utility functions diverge — redesigning incentives could realign behavior
Short-trip concentration compounds the problem
Lyft's pricing over-indexes low-revenue segments, making peak-hour share loss financially more costly than raw percentages suggest
