A   The Problem

Users were dropping off
before exploring

In SenseTime's AI-driven recommendation dialogue, product images were automatically surfaced early in the conversation. While informative, this design often disrupted the exploration flow — users would see product cards before they'd framed their intent, leading to immediate drop-off.

Hypothesis
"Delaying product exposure through progressive disclosure will increase the rate of users who make an initial interaction rather than exiting immediately."

The core insight: showing product cards too early short-circuits curiosity. Users who haven't formed a question yet don't benefit from seeing answers.

Root Cause Analysis
01 Premature product exposure Auto-surfaced product cards interrupted intent formation before users could articulate what they wanted
02 No sense of agency Users felt the conversation was "decided" before they participated — no exploration hook
03 Answers before questions In any dialogue, showing answers before a question is formed creates friction, not value
B   Experiment Design

Proposed solution &
test structure

Instead of auto-surfacing product cards, the variant required users to take one additional interaction step before product content was revealed. Minimal intervention — isolated a single UI variable.

  • Control: Product images auto-appear in first AI response
  • Variant: Products revealed only after user's first message
  • Primary metric: First-step retention — % who interact vs. immediately exit
  • Secondary: Depth of exploration, bounce-at-first-message rate

1 Problem framing Identified the friction point by tracing where users dropped off in the funnel — early exit at first AI response, before any interaction had occurred.
2 Hypothesis design Framed the test around a behavioral insight: curiosity precedes intent. Users need to form a question before an answer is useful — delaying exposure respects that sequence.
3 Metric selection Primary: first-step retention. Secondary: exploration depth and bounce rate. Avoided vanity metrics — focused on early funnel behavior change.
4 Minimal intervention No change to copy, AI logic, or product ranking — delay product card rendering by one interaction step. Isolated the UI variable cleanly for attribution.
PM Takeaway

This experiment demonstrates the full cycle: observe a problem → form a falsifiable hypothesis → design a minimal intervention → measure against a primary metric. The 32% lift came not from adding features, but from removing premature friction.

Project Outcome

SenseTime AI Chatbox is the "Evaluate" phase — using a controlled A/B experiment to validate a behavioral hypothesis about user exploration patterns in conversational AI.

The key insight generalizes beyond e-commerce: in any AI dialogue, showing answers before users have formed questions creates friction, not value.
